Assignment 5 Instructions

Develop a structured argumentative presentation on a topic of your choice. You may use any relevant sources for your evidence.

Core Requirements

  • Team size: 3–5 students
  • Position: Take a clear position on a debatable issue
  • Evidence: Use any relevant sources (no required texts from previous assignments)
  • Citations: Cite sources using Harvard style (in-slide or in-narrative)
  • Visuals: Include at least one visual (graph, chart, diagram); may use GenAI tools
  • Counterargument: Acknowledge and refute one credible counterargument
  • Teamwork: Ensure equal contribution from all team members

Format Guidelines

  • Max 8–10 minutes presentation
  • Submit slides or digital narrative link via clickUP (Turnitin-enabled)
  • Font: Arial/Times New Roman, size ≤12 | Spacing: 1.5 (for supporting documents)

Evaluation Criteria (30 marks total)

  • Clarity and strength of argument: 8 marks
  • Use of evidence from required texts: 6 marks
  • Visual aid effectiveness: 5 marks
  • Acknowledgement and rebuttal of counterarguments: 5 marks
  • Team coordination and contribution: 4 marks
  • Strong, concise conclusion: 2 marks

Extra Credit Opportunity

Include two examples of meaningful and effective translanguaging that supports both written text and visual aids.

Reflective Questions

Individual Reflection

Before collaborating as a team, consider:

  • What specific role will you take in the team, and how will you ensure your contribution is equal to others?

Team Reflection Questions

  1. How will your team structure the presentation to cover all evaluation criteria effectively?
  2. What strategies will you use to acknowledge and refute counterarguments in your presentation?
  3. How will you integrate meaningful translanguaging into both your written text and visual aids to enhance understanding for diverse audiences?

Visual Aid Guide

Effective visuals strengthen your argument. Follow these guidelines:

Relevance

Ensure visuals directly support your key points (e.g., a graph showing unemployment trends to support an argument about economic policies)

Clarity

Avoid clutter: use clear labels, legends, and concise titles. Remove unnecessary elements that don't contribute to your message.

Acknowledgement

If using AI-generated visuals (e.g., from Canva, ChatGPT), cite the tool used (e.g., "Generated using Canva AI") in the slide notes

Example Implementation

A bar chart comparing corruption levels across countries, with a caption: "Data from Transparency International (2023) shows that countries with strong anti-corruption laws (e.g., Botswana) have lower corruption scores, supporting our argument that systemic reforms are effective."

Counterargument Handling

Effectively addressing counterarguments strengthens your position. Follow this structure:

Acknowledge

Present the opposing view fairly and respectfully. "Some argue that corruption is inevitable in developing nations due to historical factors."

Refute

Provide evidence to counter it. "However, data from the World Bank (2022) shows that countries like Rwanda have significantly reduced corruption through targeted reforms, proving systemic change is possible."

Conclude

Reinforce your position. "This demonstrates that while corruption is complex, evidence-based policies can drive improvement."

Team Coordination

To ensure equal contribution:

Best Practices

  • Assign specific roles: research lead, visual designer, scriptwriter, presenter
  • Track progress: use a shared document (e.g., Google Docs) to track contributions and deadlines
  • Regular check-ins: hold team meetings to review progress and address issues
  • Peer evaluation: use evaluation forms to assess each member's input
  • Clear deadlines: set individual milestones for each component of the presentation

Example Workflow

"Each member will present a section of the argument, with the research lead providing evidence for all sections. A shared timeline ensures all tasks are completed before the deadline. Weekly check-ins will review progress and adjust as needed."

Conclusion Tips

A strong conclusion should:

Summarise Key Points

Briefly recap the main arguments without introducing new information

Restate Position

Clearly reiterate your stance on the issue

Memorable Closing

End with a powerful statement or call to action that resonates with your audience

Example Conclusion

"In conclusion, addressing unemployment requires targeted education reforms and entrepreneurship support. As evidenced by our analysis, these strategies not only reduce joblessness but also foster long-term economic resilience. Let us advocate for policy changes that empower communities and drive sustainable growth."

Extra Credit: Translanguaging

Translanguaging involves strategically using multiple languages to enhance communication. Examples:

Visual Integration

A bilingual chart where key terms are in both English and a local language (e.g., "Unemployment Rates / Ikhetho Lomhlaba" in a South African context)

Narrative Enhancement

Using a phrase in another language for emphasis, then explaining it: "The concept of 'ubuntu' (humanity towards others) underpins our argument for community-driven solutions to corruption"

Key Principle

Ensure translanguaging is purposeful and supports understanding, not just for show. Cite sources where language use is relevant to your argument.
